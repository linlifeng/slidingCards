In a nutshell

Lifeng Lin is a Chinese-born genome researcher living in the US, who is currently working as a bioinformatics scientist in the molecular diagnostics industry. He is equipped with a PhD degree in Plant Biology and programming skills from extensive trainings. He has been doing genome research since 2003 as a graduate student and later focused on oligonucleotide designs.

Autobiography

Bioinformaticians are usually either programmers with some knowledge of biology, or, a biologist with some knowledge of programming. I am the latter. I spend seven years of my graduate school mostly between the lab and the greenhouse (as I was studying cotton), doing PCRs, running gels, doing hybridizations: collecting data for my projects. In the last two years of my graduate school, I have grown an keen interesting in scripting, which had lead me to teach myself PHP and Python.

I consider myself extremely lucky to have found a job as a full time bioinformatics scientist after I got my PhD. On the job, I have been able to apply many of my genomics trainings, and more importantly, practice and improve my programming skills. During this time, I am a member of the two-person team that is responsible for the successful design of several microbiology in vitro detection panels that went on the be cleared by the FDA. Also, my partner and I have build from scratch an internal biological database server, a lab access interface, a sequencing result processing system and a pipeline that automated the probe design process.

No skill set is complete without the mentioning of "project management". It is my personal belief that the ability to oversee a project from start to finish can only be learned through real-world practice and never from books or classes alone. Although I have participated in more than a few projects, I can only honestly say that I have handled two project completely solo. The first one was a side project branched off from my PhD proposed project. I conceived the idea, did the experiment and analysis, and it went on the become an official part of my thesis and a large part of a journal publication. The second one is the automated sequencing result processing system that I developed to facilitate with company's increasing need. I am interested in design control and has been educating myself on the subject. You probably noticed that both these projects are solo projects. Yes, I am yet to be given an opportunity to be in charge of a larger project, unfortunately.

So what makes me unique? For me, my most precious asset is not in the lab trainings or the programming skills or even the management skills (which I will continue to develop), but in my non-analytical right-brain. For example: language learnings come easy for me. I have no accent in either English or Mandarin Chinese. I am getting a lot better in understanding and speaking German. I love graphical design, song-writing, photography and architecture. If there is ever a motto for me, I guess it would be “try everything.” So overall, I would probably be better suited to be called an artist who happens to have a PhD in biology and likes programming.

Lab projects

2002--Arabidopsis gene mapping

In 2002, I participated in a gene cloning project as an undergraduate research assistant. This experience trained me in basic lab techniques and genomic concepts as well as simple primer design for a targeted region.

2003--Cotton Physical Map

Continuing my research on plant genomes, I took on the cotton genome physical mapping project at University of Georgia, Plant Genome Mapping Lab. Without a reference genome to guide the assembly, the physical mapping used old-fashioned BAC-based fingerprinting method. It is a complicated and time-consuming process which involves BAC　library handling, RFLP probe design and hybridization, SSR and SSLP molecular marker design and evaluations, fingerprinting contig constructions, just to mane a few. This result was later published in BMC-Genomics and served as a reference for later cotton whole genome sequencing project.

2005--Chromosome-walking

A single gene mutation in Li2 gene resulted in a lint-less phenotype. This elicited great interest. My second project in graduate school involves the attemp to clone this gene through chromosome walking. The gene is fine-mapped to a several hundred kb region by this approach.

By the computer

Synteny and Collinearity across Plant Genomes

One particular branch of comparative genomic research deals with the conservation and evolution of gene order: the number of genome-level duplications and the reshuffling of the genome after such events. The gene order conservation is quite apparaent across monocot species; in endicot species, this research is complicated by the extensive genome duplication and gene loss events in that lineage. As I was charged with cotton genome physical map assembly without a reference genome, this genome collinearity become a powerful tool for me. This also exposed me to the wonderful world of programming and bioinformatics.

I worked with my labmate on the creation of some of the very first bioinformatics packages in dealing with synteny and collinearity searches across distantly related genomes.

Oligonucleotide thermodynamics

The major responsibility for my work at Nanosphere is to design all-inclusive, non-cross-reacting, thermodynamically-robust and multiplex-compatible oligonucleotide probes. We have developed a state-of-the-art in-house protocal and automated pipeline, ensuring that our oligos are the best in the field.

Automation of Lab-data Processing

To facilitate the product development, we developed several database-driven web application for automated processing of experimental data.
